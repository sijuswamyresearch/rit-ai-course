Part 1: Introduction to NLP

What is Natural Language Processing?

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is the field of AI that enables computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language.

Everyday Examples You Already Use: * Google Translate - Converting text between languages * Siri/Alexa - Understanding voice commands * Spam Filters - Detecting unwanted emails * Autocorrect - Predicting your next word * ChatGPT - Generating human-like responses

Why is NLP Challenging?

Human language is messy and complex:

  • Ambiguity: “I saw a man on a hill with a telescope.” (Who has the telescope?)
  • Context: “It’s cold in here.” (Could be a request to close the window)
  • Sarcasm: “Great, another meeting!” (Means the opposite)
  • Idioms: “Break a leg!” (Not literal!)

The Core Challenge: How do we convert unstructured text (words, sentences) into something a computer can process mathematically?

Part 2: The NLP Evolution

The Problem with Early Approaches

Before 2017, NLP relied on Recurrence Neural Networks (RNNs) and LSTMs.

How they worked: They processed text one word at a time, left to right, like reading a book.

The Flaws: 1. The Forgetting Problem: By the end of a long sentence, they “forgot” the beginning. Like trying to remember the first word of a paragraph after reading the whole page. 2. Slow Training: Because each word depends on the previous one, you can’t speed up the process. Training took weeks or months even on powerful computers.

The Transformer Revolution (2017)

Google researchers published a groundbreaking paper: “Attention Is All You Need”

The Key Insight: Instead of reading word-by-word, what if we could look at the entire sentence at once and figure out which words are most related to each other?

Understanding Self-Attention (The Core Idea)

Analogy: Imagine you’re reading this sentence:

“The animal didn’t cross the street because it was too wide.”

When you see the word “it”, your brain automatically asks: “What does ‘it’ refer to?” You scan the sentence and realize “it” = “the street.”

Self-Attention does the same thing mathematically: * It looks at every word in the sentence simultaneously * It calculates how strongly each word relates to every other word * It creates a “attention map” showing these relationships

Why this matters: The model can now understand context and relationships regardless of word distance!

Part 3: Large Language Models (LLMs)

What is a Large Language Model?

An LLM is a massive AI model trained on huge amounts of text data (books, websites, articles, etc.).

Examples: ChatGPT, Google Gemini, Claude, Meta Llama

How they work (simplified): 1. Training: The model reads billions of sentences and learns patterns 2. Learning Objective: Predict the next word in a sequence * Example: “The cat sat on the ___” → Model learns to predict “mat” or “couch” 3. Emergent Abilities: By learning to predict words, the model inadvertently learns: * Grammar and syntax * Facts about the world * Basic reasoning * Even some coding ability!

The Scale is Mind-Blowing

Model Parameters Training Data
GPT-3 175 billion ~45TB of text
GPT-4 Estimated trillions Even more

Parameters are like the “knobs” the model adjusts during learning. More parameters = more capacity to learn complex patterns.

Since LLMs are essentially “text completion engines,” how you ask matters!

Just ask your question directly:

Technique 3: Chain-of-Thought

Ask the model to think step by step:

Question: If I have 3 apples and buy 5 more, then give away 2, how many do I have?

Let's think step by step:
1. Start with 3 apples
2. Buy 5 more: 3 + 5 = 8 apples
3. Give away 2: 8 - 2 = 6 apples
Answer: 6 apples

Why this works: It forces the model to show its reasoning, reducing errors in math and logic problems.

Part 4: The Modern AI Stack

The Hugging Face Ecosystem

Hugging Face is like “GitHub for AI Models” - a platform where researchers and companies share pre-trained models.

Why this matters for you: * You don’t need to train models from scratch (which costs millions) * You can download and use state-of-the-art models in a few lines of code * Huge community support and documentation

Simple Example: Sentiment Analysis

Let’s see how easy it is to use a pre-trained model:

#| eval: false
from transformers import pipeline

# Load a pre-trained sentiment analysis model in ONE line
classifier = pipeline("sentiment-analysis")

# Test it
result = classifier("I love building AI models!")
print(result)
# Output: [{'label': 'POSITIVE', 'score': 0.9998}]

result = classifier("This is terrible and I hate it.")
print(result)
# Output: [{'label': 'NEGATIVE', 'score': 0.9997}]

What just happened? 1. We downloaded a model that was already trained on millions of movie reviews 2. We used it to classify text as positive or negative 3. All in 3 lines of code!

Real-World Applications

  • Customer Service: Automatically detect frustrated customers
  • Social Media Monitoring: Track brand sentiment
  • Product Reviews: Analyze customer feedback at scale
  • Content Moderation: Detect toxic or harmful content

Part 5: Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

The Hallucination Problem

LLMs have two major limitations:

1. Knowledge Cutoff: They only know what was in their training data. * GPT-4’s knowledge ends around April 2023 * It doesn’t know about recent events, new research, or your company’s latest products

2. No Access to Private Data: They can’t see your company documents, emails, or databases.

The Result: When you ask about something they don’t know, they often confidently make things up - this is called “hallucination.”

RAG: The Solution

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) combines LLMs with an external knowledge base.

How it works (3 steps):

  1. Prepare Your Documents: Convert your documents (PDFs, websites, etc.) into a searchable format
  2. Retrieve: When you ask a question, find the most relevant documents from your knowledge base
  3. Generate: Feed those documents to the LLM as context, so it can give an accurate answer

Analogy: Think of RAG as giving the LLM an “open book exam” - it can look up information before answering.

RAG in Action

Use Case: A company chatbot that answers questions about internal policies.

#| eval: false
# Simplified RAG example
from transformers import pipeline
from pinecone import PineconeClient

# Initialize the LLM and vector database
generator = pipeline("text-generation", model="gpt-3.5-turbo")
pinecone_client = PineconeClient(api_key="your-key")

def rag_query(query):
    # Step 1: Find relevant documents
    retrieved_docs = pinecone_client.query(query, top_k=3)
    context = " ".join([doc['text'] for doc in retrieved_docs])
    
    # Step 2: Generate answer using retrieved context
    response = generator(f"""
    Use the following context to answer the question.
    
    Context: {context}
    
    Question: {query}
    
    Answer:""")
    return response

# Example: Ask about company policy
print(rag_query("What is our vacation policy?"))

What’s happening: 1. The system searches your company handbook for “vacation policy” 2. It finds the relevant section 3. It gives that section to the LLM 4. The LLM generates an accurate answer based on your actual policy

Part 6: Hands-on RAG System

Building a Simple RAG Pipeline

Let’s build a basic RAG system that can answer questions about technical documentation.

Step 1: Prepare the Knowledge Base

#| eval: false
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer
import numpy as np

# Our "documents" - in reality, these would be from your PDFs, websites, etc.
documents = [
    "The learning rate for the Adam optimizer is set to 0.001.",
    "Dropout is used at 20% to prevent overfitting.",
    "The model utilizes the SiLU activation function.",
    "Training was performed on NVIDIA A100 GPUs for 48 hours.",
    "The dataset contains 50,000 training samples and 10,000 test samples."
]

Step 2: Convert Text to Vectors (Embeddings)

#| eval: false
# Load a model that converts text to numbers (embeddings)
embedder = SentenceTransformer('all-MiniLM-L6-v2')

# Convert all documents to vectors
doc_vectors = embedder.encode(documents)

print(f"Document vector shape: {doc_vectors.shape}")
# Each document is now a list of 384 numbers!

What are embeddings? * They’re numerical representations of text * Similar texts have similar vectors * We can use math to find “similar” documents

Step 3: Find Relevant Documents for a Query

#| eval: false
# The user's question
query = "What activation function is used?"

# Convert query to vector
query_vector = embedder.encode([query])

# Find the most similar document (using cosine similarity)
similarities = np.dot(doc_vectors, query_vector.T).flatten()
best_match_idx = np.argmax(similarities)

print(f"Query: {query}")
print(f"Most relevant document: {documents[best_match_idx]}")

Step 4: Generate the Final Answer

#| eval: false
# In a real system, we would now pass the retrieved document to an LLM
# to generate a natural language answer

retrieved_context = documents[best_match_idx]

# Simple template-based answer (in practice, use an LLM)
answer = f"Based on the documentation: {retrieved_context}"
print(f"Answer: {answer}")

Key Takeaways from This Demo

  1. Text → Numbers: We converted text to mathematical vectors
  2. Similarity Search: We used math to find the most relevant document
  3. Context + LLM: In a real system, we’d feed this context to an LLM for a polished answer
